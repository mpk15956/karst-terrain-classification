The Problem

Research Question

Research Question

Can Topological Data Analysis (TDA) enhance traditional geomorphology metrics for classifying surficial geology from Digital Elevation Models (DEMs) while improving computational efficiency?

Traditional geomorphometry requires expert interpretation of slope, curvature, and drainage patterns. TDA offers coordinate-free shape descriptors. Do they capture karst morphology effectively?

The Challenge & Why Karst Matters

Challenge:

  • Expensive field surveys
  • Time-consuming manual interpretation
  • Expert knowledge and resources

Why karst matters:

  • Karst aquifers provide 25% of global drinking water
  • Sinkholes cause $300M+ annual infrastructure damage in US
  • Climate change accelerates karst evolution

Traditional surficial geology mapping requires expensive field surveys, time-consuming manual interpretation, and expert knowledge. Climate change accelerates karst evolution, making automated monitoring increasingly necessary.

Opportunity & Hypothesis

  • Groundwater management and aquifer protection
  • Hazard assessment (sinkhole risk mapping)
  • Land use planning and infrastructure development
  • Large-scale automated monitoring

Hypothesis vs. Finding

Hypothesis: TDA can replace expensive multi-scale geomorphometry.

Finding: It cannot replace expensive multi-scale geomorphometry, but combining them gives best results.

Automated classification from DEMs could enable groundwater management, support hazard assessment, inform land use planning, and enable large-scale automated monitoring. This study compares four computational approaches to find the best method. We’ll explore why combining TDA with traditional methods works best.

Study Area & Dataset

Study Area: Warren & Hardin Counties, Kentucky

Mammoth Cave National Park Region

This region is ideal for karst terrain classification. The Kentucky sinkhole plain is a type locality for covered karst, with 1000+ sinkholes per km² in some areas and complex interaction of surface/subsurface drainage. High-quality 1m LiDAR DEMs capture micro-topography, and surficial geology is well-documented by USGS and state geological survey. The topologically complex terrain is ideal for testing TDA’s shape-capture ability.

  • 30,643 DEM tiles (256×256 pixels each)

    • Qat: Alluvial Terraces
    • Qc: Colluvium
    • Qca: Colluvial Accumulations
    • Qr: Residuum

Class Distribution

This is a multi-label classification problem. Tiles can have multiple classes simultaneously, for example Qal (Alluvium) combined with Qc (Colluvium).

Topology Overview

Topological Data Analysis (TDA)

TDA tracks how topological features like sinkholes and ridges appear and disappear as we filter terrain by elevation threshold.

Sinkholes are topological holes, and ridges are peaks. Long-lasting features are geologically significant, while transient features are noise.

Why Three Filtrations?

  • Elevation: valleys and depressions
  • Slope: steep areas and terrain steepness patterns
  • Superlevel: peaks and ridges

Different filtrations capture different morphology. Elevation captures valleys and depressions, slope captures steep areas and terrain steepness patterns, and superlevel captures peaks and ridges.

Detailed TDA Formalism

Simplicial Complexes: Building Blocks of Topology

Definition: An abstract simplicial complex \(K\) is a collection of subsets of vertices closed under subsets (\(\sigma \in K, \tau \subseteq \sigma \implies \tau \in K\)).

Basis Elements: A \(k\)-simplex is a subset with \(k+1\) vertices.

  • 0-Simplex: \(\{v_0\}\) (Vertex)

  • 1-Simplex: \(\{v_0, v_1\}\) (Edge)

  • 2-Simplex: \(\{v_0, v_1, v_2\}\) (Face)

For terrain: Simplices represent how elevation points connect to form topological features (sinkholes, peaks, valleys).

Betti Numbers: Counting Topological Features

Betti numbers count \(k\)-dimensional “holes” in the terrain:

  • \(\beta_0\): Connected components (separate landmasses)
  • \(\beta_1\): 1D loops (sinkholes, closed depressions)
  • \(\beta_2\): 2D voids (cavities, underground features)

Key property: Topological invariants are coordinate-free and deformation-invariant. They describe terrain shape regardless of how we measure it.

Persistent Homology: Multi-Scale Topology

Filtration: A nested sequence of complexes \(\emptyset = K_0 \subseteq K_1 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq K_n\)

Sublevel Set Filtration

Given elevation function \(f: X \to \mathbb{R}\), the sublevel set filtration is:

\[X_t = \{x \in X \mid f(x) \le t\}\]

As threshold \(t\) increases, we track how topological features (sinkholes, peaks) appear and disappear.

For terrain: Persistence measures how “significant” a feature is. Long-lasting features like deep sinkholes are more geologically important than transient ones.

Geometric Realizations: Cubical vs Simplicial Complexes

Simplicial Complex

  • Requires triangulation
  • General-purpose

Cubical Complex

  • Native to grid/DEM data
  • No artificial subdivision
  • More efficient for raster data

For DEMs: Cubical complexes avoid unnecessary triangulation, making TDA computations faster.

Experimental Design

The Spatial Autocorrelation Problem

Experimental Design: Random vs Spatial CV

Problem: Geospatial data has spatial autocorrelation. Nearby tiles are similar.

  • Random CV: mixes training and test tiles from same region
  • Spatial CV: groups tiles by geological region

Random CV mixes training and test tiles from the same region, leading to inflated performance. Spatial CV groups tiles by geological region, providing honest generalization estimates. Random CV inflates performance by approximately 7 F1 points. Spatial CV provides realistic estimates for real-world deployment.

Our Experimental Approach

  • Leave-One-Region-Out cross-validation (GroupKFold, 8 regions)
  • Random Forest (MultiOutputClassifier) with balanced class weights
  • F1-macro metric

We use Leave-One-Region-Out cross-validation with GroupKFold across 8 regions total. The classifier is Random Forest with MultiOutputClassifier wrapper and balanced class weights. F1-macro handles multi-label and imbalanced classification.

Side-by-side comparison of computational efficiency and predictive power. TDA alone underperforms traditional methods, but TDA plus Traditional combinations achieve best performance. The highest F1 score of 0.731 is not the ultra-robust method. The ultra-robust method (Multi-scale + Betti, 0.726) has the strongest statistical evidence of equivalence, though slightly lower F1.

Statistical Robustness: Ultra-Robust Methods

Ultra-Robust Methods

Three methods show near-certainty with Bonferroni-corrected p less than 0.0002 in both CV types. P-values are 200 to 8,000 times below the ultra-conservative threshold. All three combine multiscale Betti curves with multiscale derivatives. This is our strongest scientific finding, demonstrating that topological features are most effective when integrated with traditional features at multiple scales.

This hierarchy accommodates different perspectives on statistical rigor, from ultra-conservative Bonferroni to exploratory single CV type.

Computational Efficiency

Timing Comparison: TDA is 10× Faster

Computational Cost

  • TDA: ~0.085 seconds per tile
  • Traditional: ~0.981 seconds per tile
  • 10× speedup

TDA features compute in approximately 0.085 seconds per tile versus traditional methods at 0.981 seconds per tile. TDA is approximately 10 times faster than WhiteboxTools-based traditional features. Deep learning GPU inference is fast at around 0.1 seconds but requires pre-trained models and GPU resources.

Interpretation: What Worked and Why

What Each Approach Captures

Traditional:

  • Local geometry
  • Multi-scale patterns
  • Texture

TDA:

  • Global connectivity
  • Multi-scale topology
  • Shape invariance

Traditional features capture local geometry including slope steepness, curvature, and topographic position. They also capture multi-scale patterns like TPI at different radii and roughness at various scales, plus texture including surface roughness and spatial autocorrelation. TDA features capture global connectivity like sinkhole networks and drainage patterns, multi-scale topology with features that persist across elevation thresholds, and shape invariance as a coordinate-free representation of terrain structure.

Why Hybrid Works

Best combination: Multi-scale derivatives + Betti curves

  • Traditional provides local context
  • TDA captures global connectivity
  • Together: comprehensive terrain representation

This confirms that TDA enhances, not replaces, traditional features. The hybrid approach succeeds because TDA and traditional methods capture complementary information: local geometry versus global connectivity. Deep learning learns opaque patterns but doesn’t outperform, likely due to limited training data at 30k tiles, pre-trained models not specialized for terrain, and no clear advantage over interpretable features.

When to Use What?

When to Use TDA vs Traditional

Use TDA when:

  • Terrain connectivity matters
  • Compact, interpretable features needed
  • Computational efficiency important
  • Combining with traditional features feasible

Use Traditional when:

  • Local geometry most important
  • Established, well-understood metrics needed
  • Interpretability critical
  • WhiteboxTools cost acceptable

Use TDA when terrain connectivity matters, such as in karst, caves, and drainage networks. TDA is approximately 10 times faster than traditional methods. Use traditional methods when local geometry is most important, when you need established well-understood metrics, when interpretability is critical, and when WhiteboxTools computational cost is acceptable.

Hybrid Approach & What to Avoid

Use Hybrid when:

  • Resources allow combining features
  • Best possible performance needed
  • Both local and global patterns matter

Avoid:

  • Persistence images alone
  • TDA alone for karst classification

Use Hybrid TDA plus Traditional when resources allow combining features, when you want best possible performance, and when both local and global patterns matter. Avoid persistence images alone due to high dimension and low performance. Avoid TDA alone for karst classification as it provides insufficient information. Deep learning is only recommended if you have large labeled datasets and need end-to-end learning.

Limitations & Future Directions

Current Limitations

  • Spatial CV reveals lower performance
  • Hyperparameter tuning incomplete
  • Limited to Kentucky karst
  • Single filtration function tested

Spatial CV reveals lower performance. Random CV inflates metrics by approximately 7 F1 points. Hyperparameter tuning for persistence image resolution and Betti curve bins could be optimized. Results are limited to Kentucky karst and may not generalize to other regions. Only elevation and slope filtrations were tested.

Future Directions

Methodological:

  • Multi-feature filtrations
  • Spatial stratification refinement
  • Hyperparameter optimization

Alternative TDA methods:

  • Mapper algorithm, Reeb graphs, Morse-Smale complexes
  • Extended persistence, zigzag persistence, multiparameter persistence
  • Graph TDA

Application expansion:

  • Test on other karst regions
  • Apply to non-karst terrain types
  • Integrate with field survey data

Methodological improvements include multi-feature filtrations applying TDA to slope, curvature, and TWI rasters, spatial stratification refinement with better region grouping strategies, and hyperparameter optimization with systematic tuning of TDA parameters. Alternative TDA methods include Mapper algorithm, Reeb graphs, Morse-Smale complexes, extended persistence, zigzag persistence, multiparameter persistence, and Graph TDA using clique complexes on terrain networks. Application expansion includes testing on other karst regions beyond Kentucky, applying to non-karst terrain types, and integrating with field survey data for validation.

Conclusions: Practical Takeaways

Key Findings

  1. TDA alone underperforms traditional geomorphometry (F1: 0.606 vs 0.731)

  2. TDA + Traditional = Best (F1: 0.726 random, 0.663 spatial)

  3. Computational advantage: TDA is 10× faster (~0.085s vs ~0.981s per tile)

  4. Deep learning: expensive, opaque, doesn’t outperform (F1: 0.647 spatial vs 0.663 hybrid)

  5. Recommendation: Use hybrid TDA + traditional features

TDA alone underperforms traditional geomorphometry with F1 of 0.606 versus 0.731. TDA plus Traditional equals Best with F1 of 0.726 in random CV and 0.663 in spatial CV for Multi-scale plus Betti, the ultra-robust method with strongest statistical evidence. TDA is 10 times faster than traditional methods at approximately 0.085 seconds versus 0.981 seconds per tile. Deep learning is expensive, opaque, and doesn’t outperform with F1 of 0.647 spatial versus 0.663 hybrid. Recommendation is to use hybrid TDA plus traditional features for karst terrain classification. Hybrid methods provide the best accuracy with computational efficiency. TDA enhances traditional geomorphometry rather than replacing it.

Impact

  • Proof-of-concept: TDA provides efficient, interpretable features
  • Practical value: Hybrid approach offers best accuracy with reasonable cost
  • Geographic generalization: Spatial CV provides honest performance estimates

This is a proof-of-concept demonstrating that TDA provides efficient, interpretable features for terrain classification. The hybrid approach offers practical value with best accuracy and reasonable computational cost. Spatial CV provides honest performance estimates for real-world deployment and geographic generalization.
