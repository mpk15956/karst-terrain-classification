Non-Topological Compression and Acceleration of Neural Radiance Fields and 3D Gaussian Splatting: A Comprehensive Pareto Analysis1. Introduction to the Neural Rendering Computational BottleneckThe introduction of Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) and Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) established a paradigm shift in 3D computer vision, redefining the boundaries of photorealistic novel view synthesis. The fundamental architecture of a NeRF operates by mapping a continuous 5D coordinate—comprising a 3D spatial position and a 2D viewing direction—to a localized volume density and a view-dependent emitted radiance (color). While this coordinate-based multilayer perceptron (MLP) achieves extraordinary visual fidelity by learning the continuous underlying physics of scene lighting and geometry, it introduces an immense computational bottleneck. Rendering a single image requires casting rays through every pixel of the target viewpoint, sampling hundreds of points along each ray, and passing every individual point through the dense MLP. This brute-force numerical integration via volumetric ray marching results in staggering computational latency, rendering the original formulation fundamentally untenable for real-time inference, edge deployments, and hardware-constrained spatial computing applications.Consequently, the evolutionary trajectory of neural rendering over the past several years has been defined by an aggressive race toward optimal non-topological compression and acceleration. The research community has shifted its focus from merely achieving interactive frame rates to rigorously optimizing the latency-quality-size Pareto frontier. The modern challenge of model compression in this space involves the delicate balancing of the physical serialized model size on disk (measured in megabytes), the rendering speed (measured in Frames Per Second, or FPS), and the visual fidelity (quantified via metrics such as Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio, Structural Similarity Index, and Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity).Furthermore, recent systematic surveys examining spatial memory efficiency emphasize a critical, often-overlooked metric: the overhead factor, denoted mathematically as $\alpha$. The overhead factor is defined as the ratio of peak runtime memory consumed during operational inference to the saved map size written to persistent disk storage. This metric exposes the stark reality that a model with a nominally small disk footprint may still catastrophically exhaust the shared memory of an embedded Graphical Processing Unit (GPU) due to massive dynamic allocations required during the rendering pass. Independent profiling on high-end hardware, such as the NVIDIA A100 GPU, reveals that the $\alpha$ factor spans two orders of magnitude within neural methods alone, ranging from 2.3 for highly optimized systems to 215 for less efficient architectures where a mere 47 MB map requires nearly 10 GB of RAM at runtime.This report delivers an exhaustive, granular examination of the published, non-topological state-of-the-art methods for compressing, accelerating, and shrinking NeRFs, INRs, and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) models. The analysis systematically deconstructs knowledge distillation paradigms, parameter and primitive pruning strategies, precision reduction via post-training quantization, and compression-by-architecture techniques utilizing multiresolution hash grids and tensor decompositions. By synthesizing quantitative benchmarks and rigorously examining edge-specific deployments across robotics, drones, and Augmented/Virtual Reality (AR/VR) head-mounted displays, this analysis defines the contemporary Pareto frontier of real-time neural rendering.2. Establishing the Evaluation Regimes and Hardware BaselinesBefore delving into specific compression methodologies, it is necessary to establish the standard evaluation regimes, hardware configurations, and baseline metrics that anchor the quantitative comparisons within the literature. The performance of a compression technique cannot be evaluated in a vacuum; it is strictly relative to the resolution of the target imagery, the complexity of the dataset, and the specific silicon upon which the inference or training is executed.The academic community predominantly relies on two primary datasets for benchmarking. The first is the NeRF Synthetic dataset, which typically consists of bounded, object-centric scenes rendered at a standard resolution of 800×800 pixels. The second encompasses unbounded, real-world datasets such as Mip-NeRF 360 and Tanks & Temples, which feature highly complex foreground and background elements. These real-world scenes are frequently evaluated at 1080p resolutions to rigorously test the ability of the compressed model to preserve high-frequency details across vast spatial scales.Hardware baselines vary depending on the era of publication and the specific constraints being tested. Heavy offline training and foundational architectural benchmarks are frequently executed on server-grade hardware, such as arrays of NVIDIA V100s or individual RTX 3090 and A100 GPUs. For instance, a baseline vanilla NeRF trained on a single RTX 3090 might require days to converge and subsequently render novel views at less than 1 FPS at 800×800 resolution. Conversely, edge-specific evaluations intentionally handicap the available compute to measure true consumer viability, utilizing devices such as the M1 and M2 Apple Silicon in MacBook Pros, the Adreno 640 GPU in the Pixel 4, the Apple-designed GPU in the iPhone 13, and the constrained NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin for embedded robotics. Against these varied backdrops, any claim of "real-time" performance must be explicitly contextualized by the hardware driving the frames.3. Knowledge Distillation: From Volumetric Integrals to Rasterized PrimitivesKnowledge distillation in the realm of neural rendering diverges significantly from traditional classification-based network distillation. Rather than merely training a smaller MLP to mimic the activations of a larger one, rendering distillation often involves fundamentally altering the mathematical representation of the scene itself. The overarching objective is to transfer the continuous, view-dependent photorealism of a computationally expensive, offline "Teacher" NeRF into a highly optimized "Student" architecture tailored for either single-pass feedforward networks or standard graphics rasterization pipelines.3.1 Distillation to Neural Light FieldsThe most profound latency in the original NeRF formulation stems from the requirement to map a 5D coordinate to color and density via dense sampling along a ray. The R2L (Ray to Light) architecture addresses this computational bottleneck by distilling the volumetric NeRF into a Neural Light Field. A light field formulation maps an oriented, un-sampled ray directly to an RGB color output, bypassing the need for volumetric integration entirely.To overcome the inherent difficulty of learning highly complex, high-frequency light field functions from scratch, the R2L methodology employs a massive synthetic data generation strategy. The process begins by training a traditional Teacher NeRF, which typically requires around 200,000 iterations. Once optimized, this Teacher is used to synthesize a massive dataset of pseudo-images—often numbering in the tens of thousands—from entirely random viewpoints. These pseudo-renders act as dense supervision for a deep, ResNet-style MLP Student. The training of this R2L model is a highly intensive process, requiring approximately 600,000 iterations distributed across 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs.However, the inference returns are extraordinary. The resulting R2L model achieves a staggering 26x to 35x reduction in floating-point operations (FLOPs) per camera ray compared to the baseline NeRF. When evaluated on a desktop-class RTX 2080Ti or Tesla V100, the R2L methodology yields a 28x to 31x runtime speedup. Remarkably, this acceleration is achieved while simultaneously improving the rendering quality by an average of 1.4 to 2.8 dB PSNR over the original NeRF. The deeper Student network successfully leverages the Teacher's geometrically consistent priors without suffering from the Teacher's discrete sampling artifacts. On standard datasets, R2L models reach highly competitive PSNRs of 33.30 on the Fern scene and 32.80 on the Ship scene, demonstrating that light field distillation incurs negligible quality cost while virtually erasing ray-marching latency.3.2 Cross-Modal Feature and Geometry DistillationWhile R2L distills an offline model into a faster offline model, frameworks such as DistillNeRF adapt the distillation paradigm for generalized, feed-forward scene reconstruction from highly sparse inputs. Targeting outdoor autonomous driving scenarios—evaluated primarily on the nuScenes and Waymo datasets—DistillNeRF takes single-frame, multi-view camera inputs with limited overlap and predicts a rich neural scene representation without the need for test-time per-scene optimization.The distillation process implemented in DistillNeRF is two-fold and heavily reliant on prior optimization. First, dense depth maps and virtual camera targets are generated from an independently optimized, per-scene offline NeRF. The feedforward Student model utilizes these precise geometric targets to learn enhanced 3D spatial awareness from sparse, non-overlapping image inputs. Second, beyond mere geometry, semantic richness is achieved by distilling high-dimensional features from pre-trained 2D vision foundation models, specifically CLIP or DINOv2. The Student network synthesizes these inputs using a two-stage lift-splat-shoot encoder paired with a parameterized, sparse hierarchical voxel representation.The visual and functional quality cost of this cross-modal distillation is surprisingly minimal. DistillNeRF achieves a PSNR of 29.84 and an SSIM of 0.911, which slightly outperforms the offline, per-scene optimized EmerNeRF baseline (28.87 PSNR, 0.814 SSIM) after fine-tuning. More importantly, the distillation of foundation model features directly into the 3D volume unlocks zero-shot 3D semantic occupancy prediction and allows for open-vocabulary text queries across the reconstructed scene, capabilities entirely absent in standard NeRF representations.3.3 Baking to Polygon and Mesh RepresentationsTo achieve true zero-latency rendering on highly constrained mobile hardware, distillation must map neural volumes into discrete geometric primitives that are inherently supported by the hardwired rasterizers of modern GPUs. SNeRG (Sparse Neural Radiance Grid), MobileNeRF, and BakedSDF represent the vanguard of this "baking" approach.SNeRG precomputes and bakes the continuous NeRF into a sparse volumetric grid containing diffuse colors and specialized feature vectors. During the inference phase, a lightweight algorithmic ray-marcher accumulates these precomputed features along the viewing ray, and a tiny, view-dependent MLP is executed only once per pixel in a deferred rendering setup. While SNeRG dramatically increases rendering speed to real-time levels across varied hardware, it trades computational complexity for severe memory bloat. A standard SNeRG model baked at high resolution can consume well over 2.3 GB of VRAM, limiting its deployment viability on embedded devices characterized by shared memory architectures.MobileNeRF explicitly addresses SNeRG's memory bottleneck by abandoning volumetric grids entirely in favor of textured polygons. MobileNeRF extracts a mesh using a classical Z-buffer algorithm; the traditional rasterization of these polygons yields an image composed of deep features at every pixel, which are subsequently passed to a lightweight fragment shader. By storing surface textures rather than volumetric textures, MobileNeRF drastically reduces memory consumption while achieving output quality that equals or surpasses SNeRG. On a standard synthetic 360-degree object from the NeRF Synthetic dataset, MobileNeRF utilizes roughly 494,000 vertices and 224,000 faces, achieving rendering speeds comfortably above 30 FPS on standard mobile platforms such as the iPhone 13 and Pixel 4, while maintaining exceptional PSNRs around 34.09. Furthermore, MobileNeRF is fully compatible with standard WebGL environments, allowing real-time, browser-based rendering with a latency reduction of 56% to 66% compared to grid-based baselines.However, MobileNeRF meshes are not without flaws; they frequently resemble a "polygon soup," which appears optically correct from trained viewpoints but lacks physical geometric integrity and continuous surfaces. BakedSDF resolves this structural deficiency by learning a continuous Signed Distance Function (SDF) rather than a pure volumetric density field. Baking an SDF using marching cubes yields high-quality, continuous, and water-tight meshes devoid of the noise and floaters characteristic of MobileNeRF, fundamentally outperforming it in structural accuracy while remaining perfectly suitable for standard graphics rasterization.A historical barrier to these baking methods was the exorbitant training time, often requiring several hours even with multiple high-end V100 GPUs. Recent innovations such as ATM-NeRF (Accelerating Training for Mobile rendering) apply geometric regularization constraints directly to the multiresolution density grids during both the pre-training and baking stages. By utilizing the positions of 3D points projected from pre-trained posed depths, ATM-NeRF provides direct geometric supervision that yields a 30x to 70x faster training convergence compared to the baseline MobileNeRF, while preserving finer structural details in the exported mesh.4. Compression-by-Architecture: Hashed and Grid-Based EncodingsInstead of training a massive, dense MLP and subsequently attempting to prune or distill it post-hoc, compression-by-architecture strategies integrate sparsity and spatial partitioning directly into the parameterization of the scene from initialization. This approach yields models that are not only inherently smaller for storage but also orders of magnitude faster to train.4.1 Multiresolution Hash GridsThe introduction of Instant-NGP revolutionized the neural rendering field by demonstrating that the massive, slow-to-evaluate MLPs of traditional NeRFs could be almost entirely replaced by a highly optimized multiresolution hash table combined with a remarkably shallow MLP. By storing trainable feature vectors within a hash grid rather than network weights, the architecture achieves true O(1) query complexity for spatial locations. The multiresolution structure naturally disambiguates spatial hash collisions, enabling a highly compact representation that avoids the exponential parameter growth of dense grids.Instant-NGP models typically compress static scenes to a footprint of approximately 60 MB. The primary advantage of this architectural shift is the near-instant training on a single GPU. By reducing the number of floating-point operations and optimizing memory accesses, Instant-NGP accelerates NeRF reconstruction by a factor of 50 relative to the vanilla implementation without compromising visual fidelity. At an 800×800 resolution, Instant-NGP effortlessly achieves frame rates exceeding 60 FPS on standard consumer GPUs.4.2 Tensor DecompositionsTensoRF approaches architectural compression from a purely mathematical perspective, conceptualizing the radiance field as a highly dimensional 4D tensor that represents a 3D voxel grid enriched with multi-channel appearance features. To achieve profound compression, TensoRF applies advanced tensor factorization techniques—specifically Canonical Polyadic (CP) and Vector-Matrix (VM) decompositions.By decomposing the massive 3D volume into highly compact, low-rank matrix and vector components, TensoRF fundamentally bypasses the need to store dense grids. This structural compression achieves rendering quality that matches or exceeds traditional NeRF approaches while dropping the model size significantly. The algorithmic efficiency of querying factorized components reduces overall reconstruction times from days to mere minutes, placing TensoRF as a highly competitive alternative on the Pareto frontier for bounded scenes.4.3 PlenOctrees and Spatially Partitioned MLPsBefore the dominance of hash grids, PlenOctrees achieved immense inference speeds—often exceeding 150 FPS, representing a 3000x speedup over vanilla NeRFs—by fundamentally changing the rendering structure. The method trains a NeRF variant that outputs Spherical Harmonics (SH) rather than view-dependent color. These SH coefficients are then precomputed and baked into a hierarchical, 3D octree data structure. While PlenOctrees allow for direct optimization and real-time ray-marching, the architecture suffers from severe memory consumption, often requiring several gigabytes of cache on high-end hardware like the RTX 3090, thereby rendering it prohibitive for embedded applications.KiloNeRF attempted to solve this spatial partitioning problem differently by dividing the scene geometrically into a grid of thousands of tiny, independent MLPs. By querying only the specific micro-MLP corresponding to a ray's spatial location, KiloNeRF achieves a 16.21x reduction in overall size, resulting in sub-megabyte individual network weights and rendering speeds comfortably exceeding 30 FPS at 800×800 resolution.4.4 Hybrid Memory-Efficient Radiance Fields (MERF)The definitive hybrid solution for unbounded, real-world scenes was introduced by the MERF (Memory-Efficient Radiance Fields) architecture. MERF represents massive scenes using a specialized combination of high-resolution 2D feature planes and a low-resolution 3D sparse grid. This specific hybridization captures high-frequency details on the planes while utilizing the grid to model general spatial occupancy.Crucially, MERF integrates quantization-aware training (QAT) directly into its optimization pipeline. This converts its floating-point parameters into highly compressed, low-precision integer formats during the baking process with minimal geometric or photometric quality degradation. Consequently, a standard MERF model requires roughly 200 MB of VRAM to render complex unbounded scenes, standing in stark contrast to SNeRG++ representations which require up to 4.5 GB for equivalent resolution rendering. MERF thus sits at the optimal vertex of the grid-based Pareto frontier, balancing the rendering speed of PlenOctrees with the strict memory constraints of consumer mobile hardware.Furthermore, these architectures are frequently optimized using acceleration toolboxes like NerfAcc, which provide pure Python interfaces backed by highly optimized CUDA kernels to implement efficient sampling and empty-space skipping across all grid and hash-based structures, universally reducing the FLOPs required for integration.5. Pruning Strategies: From Network Weights to Spatial PrimitivesPruning strategies seek to identify and eliminate redundant parameters, shrinking model sizes and computational loads post-initialization. The application and mathematics of pruning differ vastly depending on the underlying representation: unstructured pruning for continuous MLPs versus explicit spatial primitive pruning for 3D Gaussian Splatting.5.1 Magnitude and Structured Pruning in NeRF MLPsIn traditional, continuous NeRF architectures, both unstructured magnitude pruning and structured channel pruning are leveraged to compress the network architecture. The Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) method operates under the well-documented Lottery Ticket Hypothesis, positing that dense, over-parameterized networks contain highly sparse sub-networks (winning tickets) capable of achieving identical or superior performance if trained in isolation. In the context of NeRFs, progressively removing weights of the absolute smallest magnitude over multiple iterative cycles successfully isolates these tickets, ultimately outperforming standard regularizers like variational dropout in most empirical cases.However, simple unstructured magnitude pruning is often computationally suboptimal, as it fails to consider the distinct weight distributions explicitly required for extracting fine-grained, high-frequency features across hierarchical levels in volumetric rendering. To address this structural flaw, advanced methods such as Data-independent Module-Aware Pruning (DIMAP) and joint depth-reduction optimizations have been introduced. These methodologies merge sequential convolutional blocks, eliminate unnecessary non-linearities (such as intermediate ReLUs), and rely on precise gradient saliency calculations to perform structured pruning. This results in models that reduce both run-time latency and energy consumption natively on the hardware, without triggering catastrophic degradation in the PSNR. Despite these algorithmic successes, the inherent upper bound of sequential MLP evaluation speeds eventually shifted the community's primary focus toward point-based representations.5.2 Primitive Pruning in 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)3D Gaussian Splatting achieves its acclaimed real-time rendering by completely discarding volumetric ray marching in favor of a highly parallelized, tile-based splatting of explicitly defined anisotropic Gaussians. However, a standard 3DGS scene reconstruction may generate millions of independent Gaussians, each carrying coordinate, scale, rotation, spherical harmonic (color), and opacity data. This results in gigabyte-level storage requirements and severe memory bandwidth bottlenecks during rasterization. Consequently, pruning in 3DGS involves removing the least significant physical primitives in 3D space rather than severing neural weights.Recent literature has witnessed a proliferation of heuristic, post-hoc, and fully learnable primitive pruning strategies, each occupying a distinct position on the Pareto frontier:LightGaussian: This method acts as a post-hoc filter. It computes a global significance score for each instantiated Gaussian using manual heuristics, prunes those primitives with the lowest scores, and subsequently applies vector quantization to the remaining attributes. While effective at aggressive size reduction, LightGaussian struggles fundamentally to preserve fine, high-frequency geometric details. When evaluated on the Deep Blending playroom scene, LightGaussian achieved an impressive 261.27 FPS but suffered significant photometric quality loss compared to more advanced techniques, yielding a PSNR of 27.206 on the Mip-NeRF 360 dataset.Compact3D: Seeking to improve upon post-hoc manual heuristics, Compact3D utilizes a Straight-Through Estimator (STE) to actively train a binary mask based on the evolving opacity and scale of the Gaussian parameters during optimization. While this theoretically allows the network to learn which Gaussians to drop dynamically, the discrete nature of the STE gradient approximation leads to sub-optimal convergence and noisy gradients. Compact3D achieves a PSNR of roughly 27.28 on the Mip-NeRF 360 dataset and a pruning ratio of approximately 60%.RadSplat and Mini-Splatting: These concurrent works introduce manual, heuristic-based pruning components tailored meticulously to scene visibility and density matrices. Using the newly defined RadSplat importance scores, practitioners can manually sweep pruning thresholds to find a bespoke balance between size and quality. Generating these models remains computationally expensive, typically requiring training radiance fields on an array of 8 V100 GPUs for an hour, followed by 3DGS optimization on a single A100 GPU for an additional hour.LP-3DGS (Learn-to-Prune): LP-3DGS represents the current undisputed state-of-the-art in 3DGS primitive compression. Rather than relying on manual threshold sweeps or discrete STE gradients, it implements a fully differentiable pruning framework utilizing a Gumbel-Sigmoid continuous relaxation to mathematically approximate a binary mask. This advanced formulation allows the model to automatically determine the optimal pruning ratio dynamically during a single training run, effectively reducing training optimization costs by up to 9x compared to exhaustive manual sweeping methods. Using RadSplat importance scores as a baseline constraint, LP-3DGS autonomously prunes an average of 63% of the scene's entire Gaussian population.The absolute superiority of this differentiable pruning strategy is highly evident in rigorous benchmarks. On a standard synthetic scene pruned from 2.65 million to a mere 0.265 million Gaussians, a closely related framework (PUP 3D-GS) achieved 318.06 FPS—a 4.15x speedup over the baseline—while drastically outperforming LightGaussian in rendering fidelity. On the demanding Mip-NeRF 360 and Tanks & Temples real-world datasets, LP-3DGS achieves a dominant PSNR of 27.812, soundly beating both LightGaussian (27.206) and Compact3D (27.284) while simultaneously maintaining strict parameter sparsity.6. Precision Reduction: Post-Training Quantization and Vector CodebooksQuantization shrinks models by casting high-precision floating-point numbers (FP32 or FP16) into much lower precision integer grids (typically INT8 or INT4). This mathematical truncation drastically reduces the physical model size on disk, lowers the memory bandwidth required to fetch parameters during inference, and allows specialized hardware to leverage incredibly high-throughput integer tensor cores.6.1 Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) vs. Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)Naïvely converting fully trained NeRF weights and activations directly to INT8 via standard Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) often results in catastrophic accuracy degradation. The continuous, coordinate-based nature of INRs dictates that slight quantization errors in the early layers compound spatially and exponentially through the deep network, leading to severe geometric artifacts and color banding. Experimental data confirms that uniform low-bit quantization severely degrades accuracy in sensitive, high-frequency components of implicit models. To understand why this fails, one must reference the IEEE-754 standard, where an FP32 value utilizes 1 sign bit, 8 exponent bits, and 23 mantissa bits. Forcing this vast dynamic range into 256 discrete values (INT8) crushes any outliers present in the network activations.However, nuanced experiments mapping the FP8-E4 format (an 8-bit float format with 4 exponent bits) to INT8 demonstrate that INT8 can actually exactly represent roughly 90% of the range covered by FP8-E4 without any quantization error whatsoever. By intelligently utilizing Mean Squared Error (MSE) range estimation rather than traditional min-max bounds, INT8 quantization of activations that are naturally devoid of massive outliers can occasionally improve or strictly maintain the performance metrics of the network, as the dense grid of the integer format captures the clustered data efficiently.To circumvent the inherent pitfalls of PTQ on outlier-heavy layers, Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) simulates the rounding noise of quantization directly during the forward pass of the training loop. This allows the model to proactively adapt its weight distribution via backpropagation. As explicitly observed in the MERF architecture, utilizing QAT prior to the final baking step ensures that the subsequent INT8 grid representations maintain their perceptual quality seamlessly. Advanced mixed-precision quantization frameworks further refine this process by systematically profiling network sensitivity level-by-level. They iteratively keep highly sensitive layers stored in BF16 or FP32 while aggressively forcing robust, less sensitive layers into INT8 or INT4 formats, minimizing perplexity and quality gaps.6.2 Vector Quantization (VQ)Vector Quantization departs from simple scalar integer casting by learning a discrete codebook of multidimensional feature vectors. VQ-NeRF integrates a Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE) directly into the radiance field pipeline. By applying a specialized vector quantization loss function to minimize the discrepancy between continuous spatial features and discrete codebook tokens, VQ-NeRF restricts the capacity of the model to a finite, highly optimized set of descriptors.On the complex DTU dataset, VQ-NeRF achieves a PSNR of 27.278 and an SSIM of 0.943, significantly outperforming the baseline original NeRF (22.162 PSNR) and matching advanced continuous variants like VolSDF. Furthermore, applying region-based vector quantization and K-means clustering algorithms to the explicit attributes of 3D Gaussians (such as their spherical harmonics and covariance matrices) has proven to be an essential, secondary step following magnitude pruning. Methods like CompGS-16K leverage these techniques to aggressively reduce 3DGS models to mobile-friendly sizes while maintaining LPIPS scores around 0.200.7. 3D Gaussian Splatting as the Fast NeRF AlternativeIntroduced by Kerbl et al. (2023), the original 3D Gaussian Splatting paradigm redefined the boundaries of real-time view synthesis. By modeling the scene as a vast collection of 3D anisotropic Gaussians—each parameterized by a 3D mean, a covariance matrix dictating scale and rotation, opacity, and spherical harmonics for view-dependent color—3DGS fundamentally eliminated the need for neural MLPs during inference. Instead of marching rays, 3DGS projects these 3D Gaussians into 2D space and utilizes a highly optimized, tile-based compute shader to rasterize the primitives in depth order, blending them via $\alpha$-compositing.This explicit formulation allows the original 3DGS architecture to achieve rendering speeds that exceed 100 FPS on desktop hardware at 1080p resolutions, delivering photographic realism that rivals or exceeds the best volumetric models like Mip-NeRF 360. However, this raw speed comes at the direct cost of massive storage requirements, frequently necessitating gigabytes of disk space to serialize a single unbounded scene, pushing it far outside the boundaries of embedded hardware deployment.7.1 Where 3DGS Sits on the Pareto FrontierTo properly contextualize the state of the art, it is necessary to cross-reference the leading techniques across the Latency-Quality-Size axes. Edge-Specific Deployments: Robotics, AR/VR, and Constrained SiliconThe theoretical Pareto frontier often implicitly assumes abundant parallel compute availability, benchmarking on silicon such as the NVIDIA A100, V100, or RTX 3090. However, the ultimate crucible for non-topological compression lies in actual deployments on edge devices, where severe limitations in power draw, thermal throttling, and shared memory architectures heavily dictate operational feasibility.8.1 Embedded Robotics and Drones (NVIDIA Jetson)Vision-based robotics, particularly drones and autonomous agents navigating large physical environments, face the critical issue of boundless spatial memory growth. Embedded platforms such as the NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin are severely resource-constrained, typically sharing 8 to 16 GB of unified memory dynamically between the CPU and the Ampere GPU under a strict 30W thermal power envelope.The primary barrier to edge deployment is the aforementioned overhead factor ($\alpha$). While a highly compressed neural checkpoint might occupy a mere 47 MB on a solid-state drive, instantiation and the subsequent gradient backpropagation or volume rendering recursion can cause peak runtime memory to explode unpredictably. Profiling indicates that $\alpha$ can range from 2.3 to 215 depending entirely on the model's memory architecture. If a model requests 10 GB of RAM at runtime for rendering buffers, it will immediately trigger out-of-memory kernel errors on an 8 GB Jetson module, rendering its disk compression entirely irrelevant.While 3DGS boasts immense FPS on desktop GPUs, its explicit mathematical formulation demands vast, highly irregular memory accesses for tile-based parallel rasterization. This access pattern completely overwhelms the Jetson Orin's limited memory bandwidth when attempting to process millions of Gaussians. To combat this silicon-specific bottleneck, specialized algorithms like LS-Gaussian, Potamoi, and AdR-Gaussian have been developed explicitly to bypass bottlenecks in the mobile Ampere GPU rasterization pipeline. By implementing advanced Level-of-Detail (LoD) search algorithms that actively exploit temporal coherence across consecutive frames, these frameworks avoid repetitive octree traversals, successfully rendering city-scale 3DGS models on the Jetson Orin at viable, uninterrupted frame rates. Furthermore, algorithm-hardware co-designed accelerators, such as the proposed GauSPU processor, demonstrate that physically handling the irregular memory access of 3DGS via dedicated silicon can achieve real-time SLAM with a mere 2.23% power overhead relative to traditional GPU constraints.8.2 AR/VR and Head-Mounted DisplaysIn the domain of Augmented and Virtual Reality, maintaining deep user immersion and preventing motion sickness fundamentally requires sustained, unwavering high frame rates (typically 72 to 120 FPS) and ultra-low motion-to-photon latency. Standalone consumer devices, such as the Meta Quest 3 (powered by the Qualcomm Adreno 740 GPU) and the Microsoft HoloLens 2, present a uniquely difficult engineering challenge due to their extreme thermal limitations and strict reliance on standard, standardized graphics APIs like Vulkan and DirectX 12.In these highly constrained, untethered environments, traditional volumetric NeRFs are wholly unsuited. Methods like MobileNeRF and BakedSDF dominate this specific edge paradigm. By completely distilling complex neural representations into textured polygon meshes and standard Z-buffer formats, these models interface flawlessly with the hardwired rasterization pipelines of mobile GPUs, maximizing the hardware's native efficiency without requiring custom CUDA implementations.Alternatively, when maximum, uncompromising visual fidelity is mandated by the application, asymmetric client-server setups are frequently employed. In this distributed architecture, a powerful, tethered edge workstation (e.g., equipped with an Intel Core i7 and an RTX 3080 Ti with 12 GB VRAM) handles the heavy 3DGS reconstruction and compression locally. The server continuously streams bounded, heavily compressed Gaussian replicas directly to the headset (such as the Meta Quest 3 via Oculus Link, or the HoloLens 2) where highly localized rendering occurs using interactive clipping techniques. This hybrid streaming approach, combined with aggressive LP-3DGS compression, ensures that the cognitive and thermal load on the user's headset remains safely low without ever exceeding the device's standalone compute capabilities.9. Synthesis and Future TrajectoriesThe intricate landscape of NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting compression has evolved with remarkable speed, transitioning from naive, unstructured MLP weight pruning to highly sophisticated architectural redesigns and primitive-based continuous distillation. The current non-topological state of the art represents a profound engineering triumph of harmonizing deep learning with traditional, hardwired graphics pipelines.The empirical data across the literature unequivocally shows that no single rendering paradigm universally dominates the Pareto frontier; rather, supremacy is contingent entirely upon the specific hardware target and the acceptable memory overhead. For pure inference latency reduction and seamless integration into existing commercial game engines, polygon distillation methods like MobileNeRF and BakedSDF remain unparalleled in their utility. For memory-constrained, high-fidelity browser rendering, grid-hybrid architectures like MERF optimally balance tight VRAM limitations with impressive frame rates through quantization-aware training.However, the most profound and rapidly accelerating advancements currently reside in the 3DGS domain. Differentiable learn-to-prune frameworks, powerfully exemplified by LP-3DGS, successfully mitigate the historical storage bloat of explicit point-based representations, mathematically cutting Gaussian counts by over 60% while maintaining exceptional PSNR and yielding 300+ FPS on standard consumer hardware. As un-tethered edge deployments on platforms like the Jetson AGX Orin and Meta Quest 3 become commercially ubiquitous, the focus of the community must continue pivoting toward minimizing the runtime overhead factor ($\alpha$). Future advancements will undoubtedly converge on deeper hardware-aware quantization and spatio-temporal coherence algorithms, permanently embedding real-time, photorealistic neural rendering into the fabric of mobile robotics and spatial computing.
