Topological Regularization in Implicit Neural Representations and Neural Radiance Fields: A Comprehensive Analysis of Recent BreakthroughsThe Paradigm Shift in Continuous Spatial ModelingThe assessment that topological regularization for Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) and Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) was practically nonexistent—or at least computationally unfeasible—prior to mid-2025 is highly accurate. Historically, the reconstruction and modeling of continuous physical phenomena have relied on discrete, grid-based architectures such as pixel lattices, audio waveforms, and volumetric voxel grids. While these discrete methodologies formed the bedrock of modern sensing, storage, and reconstruction pipelines, they inherently separated the digital representation from the intrinsic continuity of the physical world. Implicit Neural Representations fundamentally disrupted this paradigm by modeling signals as continuous functions parameterized by the weights of a coordinate-based multilayer perceptron (MLP).The introduction of NeRFs, and subsequently 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), unlocked unprecedented capabilities in high-fidelity novel view synthesis, dynamic scene reconstruction, and generative 3D modeling. However, operating within continuous, unbounded coordinate spaces introduced a profound secondary challenge: ensuring topological correctness. Because standard NeRF models evaluate volume density independently along rendering rays and rely primarily on localized photometric reconstruction losses, they completely lack global structural awareness. The optimization landscapes governing these fields are heavily under-constrained. Consequently, generated 3D structures frequently exhibited severe topological artifacts, including floating geometric fragments ("floaters"), spurious disconnected components, self-intersections during dynamic deformation, and the erroneous closure of essential structural voids.For several years, imposing explicit topological constraints on INRs was considered mathematically and computationally prohibitive. Classical topological evaluation relies on combinatorial, discrete mathematics, which is fundamentally incompatible with the continuous, gradient-based optimization required by neural networks. However, a critical inflection point occurred between the NeurIPS 2025 and ICLR 2026 conferences. Catalyzed by breakthroughs in scalable Persistent Homology (PH) and differentiable topological layers, researchers successfully bridged the gap between Algebraic Topology and deep neural rendering.This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level examination of the state-of-the-art in topological regularization for INRs, NeRFs, and 3DGS. It addresses the mathematical foundations of differentiable topology, explores the algorithmic innovations that dismantled historic computational bottlenecks, analyzes the architectural integration of structural priors into contemporary rendering pipelines, and evaluates the far-reaching implications of these constraints across generative modeling, physics-informed design, and medical diagnostics.Mathematical Foundations: Translating Discrete Topology to Continuous GradientsThe integration of topological constraints into continuous neural optimization requires translating global structural properties—which are inherently discrete—into differentiable loss functions. This translation is primarily achieved through the framework of Topological Data Analysis (TDA), specifically utilizing Persistent Homology.Persistent Homology, Filtrations, and Betti NumbersPersistent homology provides a robust, multi-scale descriptor of a manifold's intrinsic shape by capturing topological invariants that persist across various scales, offering a geometry-agnostic representation of data. To evaluate the topology of a coordinate-based point cloud or a sampled implicit density field, the continuous space must first be represented as a parameterized family of simplicial complexes (e.g., Vietoris-Rips, Čech, or Alpha complexes), which are indexed by a spatial scale parameter $r$. As the parameter $r$ increases, simplices are sequentially added, forming a mathematical filtration.During this filtration process, specific topological features are born (appear) and eventually die (are filled in or merged with other structures). These features are formalized as homology groups, denoted as $H_k$, where the dimension $k$ defines the specific nature of the topological feature.Homology Group (Hk​)Topological Feature DescriptionCorresponding Betti Number (βk​)Geometric Interpretation in 3D Space$H_0$Connected components$\beta_0$The number of isolated objects, fragments, or clusters.$H_1$One-dimensional loops$\beta_1$The number of rings, handles, cycles, or tunnels.$H_2$Two-dimensional voids$\beta_2$The number of enclosed cavities or hollow spaces.The rank of each homology group, known as the Betti number ($\beta_k$), quantifies the number of independent features of that specific dimension. For any given mesh or simplicial complex, these invariants relate directly to the Euler characteristic ($\chi$) via the identity $\beta_0 - \beta_1 + \beta_2 = |V| - |E| + |F|$, where $V, E$, and $F$ denote the number of vertices, edges, and faces, respectively. The lifespan of each feature across the filtration is plotted on a persistence diagram as a coordinate pair representing (birth, death). Features residing close to the diagonal of the persistence diagram represent transient topological noise (e.g., surface roughness or rendering artifacts), while those positioned far from the diagonal indicate robust, fundamental structural properties of the underlying geometry.Resolving the Non-Differentiability BarrierIncorporating persistent homology into the backpropagation routine of an INR poses a massive theoretical hurdle: changes in Betti numbers and persistence diagrams represent discrete, combinatorial jumps rather than smooth, continuous variables. For instance, if a gradient descent step causes two disconnected components to merge, the $\beta_0$ value drops instantaneously from 2 to 1. This step function provides no useful local gradient to guide the neural network weights.To construct a valid "topological loss," researchers engineered differentiable topology layers that map these discrete topological events back to the continuous parameters of the neural network. This is achieved by utilizing barcode calculus, soft combinatorics, and subgradient regimes.By defining dynamic weighting strategies, the operations required to compute the persistence barcode are rendered piecewise differentiable. Advanced differentiable algorithms compute the gradient of a persistence diagram with respect to the input function values—such as the Signed Distance Field (SDF) values predicted by an INR—via continuous non-linear optimization. Consequently, if a NeRF produces an unwanted disconnected component (an erroneous $H_0$ feature) due to photometric ambiguity, the topological loss isolates the specific coordinate samples that caused the feature's birth. It then calculates a subgradient penalty that forces the implicit network to either bridge the spatial gap or entirely suppress the artifact's density.Distance Metrics in Persistence SpaceTo supervise network training, the distance between the persistence diagram of the generated neural field and a target topological prior must be quantified. Modern frameworks leverage Wasserstein distances, Sliced Wasserstein approximations, or the Maximum Correntropy Criterion between persistence diagrams. This topological loss is typically integrated as a weighted auxiliary term alongside the primary reconstruction loss (e.g., $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{photometric}} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{\text{topo}}$). Adaptive topological losses further refine this by employing reparameterized cross-entropy weighting strategies, where negative sample weights are scaled exponentially based on their topological divergence from the expected structure.Surmounting Computational Bottlenecks: Scalability Breakthroughs (2025-2026)Even with theoretical differentiability established, the integration of topological constraints into implicit fields fundamentally clashed with the computational complexity of standard Algebraic Topology algorithms. Computing the persistence diagram for a Vietoris-Rips complex over $n$ points scales at $O(n^2)$ to $O(n^3)$ in the worst-case scenarios. For Neural Radiance Fields or high-resolution 3D Gaussian Splatting scenes containing millions of sampled coordinate points, $O(n^3)$ complexity rendered standard persistent homology completely mathematically intractable for per-step gradient descent updates.The landscape fundamentally shifted between late 2025 and 2026, as researchers deployed novel algorithmic paradigms to break this bottleneck, effectively bridging the gap between topological rigor and the vast scale of neural rendering. The researchers directly addressed the exponential growth of standard simplicial complexes by developing a highly scalable alternative to the computationally expensive Alpha and Witness complexes.Informally, the Flood complex operates at a given filtration value $r \geq 0$ by selecting all simplices from a Delaunay triangulation of a small, computationally viable subset of the ambient point cloud $X$. These simplices are deemed valid and added to the complex only if they are fully covered by mathematical balls of radius $r$ emanating from the larger, dense set $X$—a process termed "flooding". Because this process drastically limits the number of edges and faces required for computation without corrupting the underlying homology, the algorithm can efficiently process several millions of points.Crucially, the authors provided stringent theoretical guarantees on the approximation quality, proving stability and bounded over-approximation error (achieving as low as 3% variance compared to exhaustive baseline sampling). The Flood complex is fully amenable to GPU parallelization and provides the required efficiency to apply PH-based losses to massive 3D Gaussian Splat populations and dense NeRF ray-samplings. The framework's ability to maintain high topological precision while slashing computational overhead was the primary enabler for incorporating deep topological learning into photorealistic scene representations.Principal Persistence Measures (ICLR 2026)Further advancing the scalability and stability of topological loss, an ICLR 2026 submission concerning Principal Persistence Measures targeted the specific issue of gradient discontinuities and training instability. Standard PH, when used as a regularizer, often causes erratic and hostile loss landscapes due to sudden jumps in the gradient vector when a persistent feature dies or merges during training.To resolve this instability, Principal Persistence Measures compute the persistent homology over a large number of small, overlapping subsamples of the density field, rather than attempting to evaluate the entire global field in a single pass. By evaluating these highly parallelized measures across the GPU and aggregating the results, the topological regularizer produces gradients that are mathematically proven to be continuous for smooth density fields. This continuous gradient flow actively prevents the unstable training behaviors typically seen in adversarial tasks or high-variance NeRF optimizations. Consequently, Principal Persistence Measures offer a smooth, highly stable topological regularizer that scales seamlessly to multi-million-parameter INRs.Topological Regularization in Static NeRFs and Implicit SurfacesWith computational scaling resolved, topological constraints were rapidly adopted to mitigate the fundamental ambiguities of Neural Radiance Fields. Because standard NeRF reconstruction relies almost exclusively on photometric loss (e.g., matching the pixel color of the rendered ray to the ground truth 2D image), the network is virtually blind to internal structures that are either visually occluded or lack distinct textural variation across views. This localized optimization manifests as "floaters"—small, high-density clouds of coordinate points suspended in empty space—or erroneous holes where solid surfaces should exist.The introduction of topological regularization addresses this flaw by enforcing a global prior on the continuity of the underlying structure. A seminal approach in this domain is STITCH (Surface Reconstruction using Implicit Neural Representations with Topology Constraints and Persistent Homology), published in late 2024 and highly influential through 2025. STITCH integrates differentiable topological data analysis tools directly into the neural implicit surface reconstruction pipeline. By applying a PH-based loss function, the model enforces the specific geometric prior of a single connected 2-manifold object.If the NeRF optimization attempts to generate a disjointed artifact (which would incorrectly increase $\beta_0$) in order to artificially lower the photometric loss in a noisy or unobserved region, the topological loss immediately identifies the anomaly and heavily penalizes the creation of that isolated component. Theoretical analyses accompanying the STITCH framework demonstrate that optimizing this specific topology loss via stochastic subgradient descent strictly guarantees mathematical convergence to a single connected component.Further advancements include the GSDF architecture, a dual-branch system combining 3D Gaussian Splatting and neural Signed Distance Fields (SDF). This approach leverages mutual guidance and joint supervision, using the implicit SDF to provide smooth, topologically sound surfaces, while the Gaussian primitives handle high-frequency textural details. Other models optimize the deformation of unoccluded regions using the maximum correntropy criterion combined with unsupervised implicit neural representations, avoiding collapsed, tearing, and physically implausible surface results.Dynamic View Synthesis (4D) and Temporal TopologyWhile topological regularization greatly enhances static scenes, its impact on 4D (dynamic) reconstruction is profoundly transformative. Traditional explicit deformation models (such as SMPL meshes for human avatars) struggle to handle large topological discrepancies or non-human subjects. Conversely, 4D implicit models—which represent dynamic sequences as spatiotemporal functions—frequently yield physically implausible results during complex motion. Without topological awareness, implicit dynamic models often allow limbs to freely intersect the torso or objects to momentarily merge and detach, as the photometric loss cannot differentiate between occlusion and actual volumetric intersection.The 4DRecons framework (4D Neural Implicit Deformable Objects Reconstruction) fundamentally altered dynamic optimization by introducing a topology regularization term that strictly enforces a fixed topology over time. This approach links the continuous vertices of a 3D shape across the temporal sequence with topological features mapped on a persistent diagram. The regularization acts as a strict temporal invariant constraint: the total number of topological features (represented by $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$) on the persistent diagram must remain exactly constant across all frames $t_1$ to $t_n$.By enforcing this persistent homology loss, 4DRecons elegantly penalizes inter-penetrations and self-intersections. When an arm passes through a torso in an unconstrained implicit field, it creates an artificial handle or alters the sequence of connected components, causing an immediate, massive spike in the topological loss. 4DRecons is thus recognized as the first monocular dynamic reconstruction approach to enforce strict topological consistency across implicit fields.In parallel, hybrid architectures like DreamMesh4D (NeurIPS 2024) bind Gaussian splats to the triangle faces of a mesh to enable the differentiable optimization of both texture and dynamic geometry. This framework utilizes a geometric skinning algorithm that combines Linear Blending Skinning (LBS) and Dual-Quaternion Skinning (DQS) to drive motion. To maintain structural integrity during these deformations, DreamMesh4D employs an As-Rigid-As-Possible (ARAP) topological loss, which leverages the explicit topology of the base mesh to enforce local rigid constraints and spatial-temporal consistency across the 4D sequence. Furthermore, frameworks utilizing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for locality-sensitive avatars have formally proven the topological guarantees of non-rigid motion models, preserving fine-grained kinematic details while keeping pose information strictly local to neighboring body structures.3D Gaussian Splatting: Bridging Explicit and Implicit TopologyWhile NeRFs parameterize the scene via continuous MLPs, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) represents scenes using a discrete set of anisotropic, volumetric Gaussians. 3DGS offers rapid training and highly efficient real-time tile-based rasterization. However, its purely unstructured, discrete nature makes it highly susceptible to catastrophic geometric fragmentation and structural collapse during physical simulation, editing, or shape morphing. Directly interpolating these discrete primitives leads to a breakdown of structural integrity precisely because they lack explicit topological priors connecting them.To solve this vulnerability, researchers developed Vertex-Gaussian topology regularization, prominently utilized in the recent THOM (Text-to-Hand-Object Mesh) framework. This regularization technique elegantly bridges the gap between unstructured volumetric representations and explicit mesh topology.The mechanism begins by establishing a precise, one-to-one mapping between the vertices of an explicitly extracted mesh (generated via Poisson reconstruction and farthest point sampling upsampled to roughly 400,000 vertices) and the individual 3D Gaussians. Because Score Distillation Sampling (SDS)—which is often used to guide 3DGS generation from Vision-Language Models—is vastly under-constrained, the resulting Gaussians naturally drift into jittery, fragmented surfaces with degraded colors.THOM remedies this by applying a Laplacian regularization operator, denoted as $\mathbf{L}(\mathbf{x})$, to three core Gaussian parameters: positions ($\mu$), colors ($c$), and scales ($s$). The Laplacian operator enforces local smoothness by leveraging the established vertex adjacency topology, mathematically expressed as:$$\mathbf{L}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} - \sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbf{x}_{(\cdot ,j_{k})}\mathbf{w}_{(\cdot ,k)}$$where $\mathbf{w}$ represents the adjacency-weight vector for the top-$K$ nearest neighbors in the topological graph.For the Gaussian positions ($\mu$), the framework minimizes the difference between the Laplacian of the Gaussians and the Laplacian of the reference mesh vertices ($\mathbf{V}$). For colors ($c$) and scales ($s$), their respective Laplacians are minimized directly. The application of this topological constraint guarantees visual fidelity, prevents erratic shading artifacts, and ensures the scale of the Gaussians remains smooth across the object's surface. Crucially, the geometric correspondence maintained by this topological regularization enables accurate physical computations, such as collision detection and penetration avoidance during complex, dynamic hand-object interactions.Similarly, the TopoGaussian framework (ICLR 2026) infers internal topology structures from visual clues by marrying 3DGS with a highly versatile, particle-based differentiable simulator. By utilizing gradients derived from a simulator that accommodates constitutive models, actuator dynamics, and collisions, TopoGaussian applies topological representations for deep optimization without interfering directly with a rigid boundary mesh, allowing it to recover complex internal manifold structures from purely opaque surfaces.Generative Models and the Injection of Diffusion PriorsThe rapid evolution of INRs and NeRFs is intrinsically linked to the concurrent rise of generative AI, particularly diffusion models. Diffusion models are now heavily utilized to generate 3D assets and dynamic scenes from textual prompts via SDS. However, generative 3D processes exhibit a severe vulnerability: while a diffusion model might master the local textures and superficial appearance of an object, it lacks an explicit understanding of global topology.TopoDiffusionNet (ICLR 2025)The limitations of generative topologies were explicitly addressed by the ICLR 2025 publication TopoDiffusionNet (TDN): A Topology-aware Diffusion Model. Standard diffusion models operate by reversing a stochastic noise process via score matching. However, the mechanism of adding and removing Gaussian noise operates independently of the structural coherence of the data manifold. As a result, diffusion models frequently fail to satisfy basic Betti number constraints, leading to outputs such as a coffee mug generated without a proper handle, a bicycle with three disconnected wheels, or human avatars with merged limbs.TopoDiffusionNet rectifies this by integrating persistent homology directly into the intermediate denoising steps of the diffusion model. During reverse diffusion, TDN extracts topological structures from the noisy manifold representation. A topology-based objective function is then evaluated against a specified Betti number prior. By guiding the denoising score estimator with this topological loss, TDN actively preserves intended structural loops and connected components while forcefully suppressing topologically destructive noise patterns.The success of TDN represents a critical junction for INRs. Because Score Distillation Sampling from 2D diffusion models is the primary method for training zero-shot Text-to-3D NeRFs, injecting topological awareness into the base diffusion model directly curtails the generation of floating artifacts and irregular manifolds before the NeRF optimization process even encounters them.Generative Design and Structural SynthesisBeyond view synthesis, generative topological modeling is heavily utilized in complex 3D shape synthesis. The TopoGen framework extracts both discrete Betti numbers and continuous persistence points to robustly characterize 3D shapes. By using these features as conditional guidance in generative networks, TopoGen achieves precise topology-aware generation from sparse and partial point clouds. This allows users to explicitly control and alter the topology of generated representations simply by modifying the persistence points directly.Similarly, the TAGG (Topology-aware Graph Diffusion Model with Persistent Homology) framework employs a Persistence Diagram Matching (PDM) loss to guarantee high topological fidelity during the diffusion process. This forces synthesized network structures to strictly align with the observed distribution of topological features, preventing the loss of vital structural nuances often observed in conventional graph convolutional networks.Interdisciplinary Implementations: Medical Diagnostics and Physics-Informed EngineeringThe necessity for absolute topological fidelity extends far beyond visual rendering and virtual reality into domains where geometric errors have catastrophic, real-world consequences, such as medical diagnostics and structural engineering. The maturation of topological regularization in INRs has enabled profound advancements in these critical fields.Medical Segmentation and Anatomical FidelityIn medical imaging, topological errors are not merely visual artifacts; they are severe diagnostic failures. An implicit model segmenting a pulmonary airway or a neurovascular network might achieve a high volumetric overlap metric (e.g., a high Dice score) while completely severing a critical blood vessel or airway branch in its prediction. Traditional voxel-based models inherently lack the geometric machinery to maintain faithful topology and curvature, which is critical for accurate measurement of features like cortical thickness.The introduction of INRs equipped with differentiable topology layers resolves this fatal flaw. Continuous implicit representations, heavily guided by persistent homology loss, enforce the unbroken continuity of vascular networks and the strict single-connected-component requirement of organs.The AG-TAL (Anatomically-Guided Topology-Aware Loss) formulation was designed explicitly to handle 3D multiclass settings, such as segmenting the Circle of Willis (CoW) in neurovascular imaging. By combining connected-component analysis with hole-detection penalties and persistent homology approximations, these loss functions preserve complex, high-genus vascular topology and significantly reduce inter-class misclassification and catastrophic disconnections. Furthermore, frameworks such as TopoCon-MP extract multi-parameter topological signatures to capture the deep multiscale structures of skin lesions (such as melanoma), massively outperforming standard vision transformers that rely solely on superficial appearance cues.In the domain of dental imaging, topology-constrained quantized networks (e.g., nnUNet variants) utilize a joint optimization objective that harmonizes standard cross-entropy loss with topological constraints. By computing differentiable persistent homology features through probabilistic approximations of topological invariants, these networks achieve clinically plausible segmentations on dental CBCT scans while retaining the hardware efficiency required for rapid deployment. Additionally, novel applications of "border irregularity loss" combined with topological constraints have shown unprecedented success in the automated segmentation of highly variable primary brain lymphomas on post-contrast MRIs.Physics-Informed Topology Optimization (IF-TONIR)In the domain of structural engineering and physics, topology optimization seeks to distribute material within a highly constrained design space to maximize performance (e.g., structural stiffness or fluidic flow) under specified boundary conditions. Standard deep learning approaches for this task relied heavily on image-based boundary conditions and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), which resulted in severe domain and resolution dependencies.The IF-TONIR (Iteration-Free Topology Optimization based on Implicit Neural Representations) framework revolutionized this design process. By utilizing continuous implicit fields parameterized by MLPs, IF-TONIR creates a completely resolution-free optimization environment. The integration of Persistent Homology into this pipeline allows the network to assess the global connectivity and micro-voids of the generated porous structures, such as Triply Periodic Minimal Surfaces (TPMS). By dynamically minimizing the mathematical distance between the persistence diagrams of the generated structures and an optimal theoretical prior, the network significantly improves macro-connectivity and structural stiffness compared to standard generative models.Through the use of Fourier-encoded coordinates and differentiable topological loss, IF-TONIR accurately resolves complex boundary transitions, completely removing the need for iterative, computationally expensive Finite Element Analysis (FEA) during the design inference phase. Similar topological constraints have been applied in celestial mechanics via MasconCubes, which utilize INRs to explicitly model mass distributions and gravitational constraints for asteroids, achieving computational speeds 40 times faster than prior geometric networks.Adversarial Robustness and Topological CompressionAn underexplored but increasingly critical outcome of topological regularization is its profound impact on model robustness and adversarial defense. Standard geometric and pixel-level adversarial attacks against 3D point clouds and image classifiers manipulate point locations to fool the network without significantly altering the human-perceived global shape.However, researchers have identified that the homological structure of the data manifold serves as an incredibly robust fingerprint that is exceedingly difficult for adversarial attacks to forge. In 2026, investigations into the geometry of Large Language Models (LLMs) and INRs revealed that adversarial inputs (e.g., indirect prompt injection and backdoor fine-tuning) frequently induce a phenomenon known as "topological compression". Under an adversarial attack, the latent space representation of the network collapses, losing its complex, high-dimensional void and loop structures, rendering the latent space structurally simpler and highly compressed.By enforcing a topological loss or implementing topology-aware attention modules, networks preserve their archetypal structural geometry. Consequently, models utilizing persistent homology are inherently more resilient to adversarial noise, as the topological invariants ($\beta_0, \beta_1$) remain stable under minor spatial perturbations. This reveals that topological regularization serves a vital dual purpose: enforcing geometric fidelity in generative spatial outputs while fortifying the latent representations of discriminant and language models against malicious latent space collapse.Synthesis and Concluding PerspectivesThe pursuit of high-fidelity, structurally coherent Implicit Neural Representations has fundamentally shifted from purely photometric heuristics toward rigorous, topology-aware optimization. The premise that topological regularization was largely absent from NeRF and INR optimization prior to mid-2025 has been validated; however, the landscape has been entirely redefined by a cascade of mathematical and architectural breakthroughs occurring throughout late 2025 and early 2026.The primary barrier to this integration—the discrete, non-differentiable nature of topological invariants and the severe $O(n^3)$ computational scaling of persistent homology—has been systematically dismantled. Differentiable topology layers utilizing barcode calculus and subgradient regimes have allowed Betti numbers to operate directly as backpropagated loss constraints. Simultaneously, algorithmic innovations such as The Flood Complex and Principal Persistence Measures have enabled these calculations to scale gracefully to the massive, multi-million point clouds inherent to NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting architectures.Frameworks like STITCH and 4DRecons have successfully utilized persistent homology to enforce connected-component priors and prevent dynamic self-intersections in complex 4D volumetric fields. Concurrently, the THOM architecture has demonstrated that explicit vertex-to-Gaussian mapping, combined with local Laplacian regularization, provides the structural rigidity required to prevent the catastrophic fragmentation of 3DGS under generative Score Distillation Sampling. Furthermore, the embedding of persistent homology directly into the diffusion denoising sequence, as seen in TopoDiffusionNet, ensures that the foundational generation of these fields respects intrinsic topological manifolds from the ground up, stopping generative hallucinations at their source.The integration of Algebraic Topology into neural rendering is no longer a peripheral or mathematically intractable concept; it has become a central inductive bias. By forcing neural networks to respect the fundamental rules of connectivity, continuous deformation, and spatial integrity, topological regularization elevates INRs from sophisticated view-interpolators to physically plausible, geometrically robust representations of the 3D world. This evolution secures the future of INRs not just in visual media, but in safety-critical domains ranging from medical diagnostics to physics-informed structural engineering.
